GT Off Road

Overview

I joined Georgia Tech's off-road team partway through my first semester of college after being introduced to it by a friend. I quickly found a home amongst the electrical and computer engineering oreinted Data Aquisition team (DAQ), and will be serving as the next DAQ team lead starting next year. I still have much to learn about how both the car and our tech works, but I'm excited for what my future with the team will look like.

Data Aquisition

During my time at DAQ I have personally worked on improving our radio range through the installation of a larger antenna, helped with the addition of Hall-effect sensors on the front wheels, researched the use of a mesh network to improve the range of our data transmission set up, started developing a data distribution server to facilitate easier access to live data, built the battlestation (our data collection computer), and contributed in some way or another to almost everything the team has worked on over this last semester. I'm excited to take over as team lead next year, and plan on improving DAQ's documentation procedures and finishing the development and deployment of the projects listed above.

Personal Contribution

The Data Aquisition team exists to generate and feed data to all of the other teams in Off Road. This is accomplished by connecting a multide of different types of sensors to the car and transmiting their data back to what we've loving come to call the "Battlestation." The AUX DAQ (a small circuit board mounted on the car to handle data collection and transmission) makes all of this possible through the use of a custom transmission protocol designed by previous DAQ members. All of this tech is designed to take a bruising, contending with everything from high velocity impacts to mud splashes for hours on end during races.
