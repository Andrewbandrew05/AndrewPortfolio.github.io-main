GT Off Road

Overview

I joined Georgia Tech's off-road team partway through my first semester of college after being introduced to it by a friend. I quickly found a home amongst the electrical and computer engineering oriented Data Acquisition team (DAQ), and took over as team lead at the beginning of my sophomore year. I absolutely love the explorative freedom that DAQ offers its members, and have leveraged this awesome attribute to recruit an almost entirely new team after almost all of the older members aged out.

Freshman Year

During my freshman year I worked on improving our radio range through the installation of a larger antenna, helped with the addition of Hall-effect sensors on the front wheels, researched the use of a mesh network to improve the range of our data transmission set up, built a new base-station, and laid the groundwork for a complete revamp of the data collection system. These innovations led to my appointment as team lead in addition to having a real impact on the efficacy of the old DAQ setup.

Sophmore Year

Under my leadership DAQ has increased the polling rate of our sensors by over 3000x, revamped our documentation, and designed a new testing setup for sled pulls. I’ve directed my members to research and build a discrete wiring harness to improve data resolution and simplify the addition of sensors, reached out to previous DAQ leads to help with a PCB redesign for the Aux Daq itself (the piece of hardware that handles on-car data collection), and brought an industrial engineering friend of mine on board to help streamline many of the administrative tasks. These improvements have vastly increased the efficiency and effectiveness of our data collection solutions, aiding Off Road’s mechanical and design teams in their never-ending quest for speed.
